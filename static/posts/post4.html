Election Season Deepfake Surge: 17 Videos We Flagged This Week

We scanned 50+ political YouTube videos in 7 days. 17 scored 85%+ AI-generated. Here's what we found — and how to spot them yourself.

March 15, 2026 7 min read Deepfake News

In the 7 days before the March 15 primary elections, dawg.city flagged 17 political videos as 85%+ AI-generated. We scanned over 50 in total. The pattern was unmistakable — and alarming.

Here's a breakdown of what we found, the tactics being used, and how to protect yourself during election season.

What We Scanned

Our team ran 50+ political YouTube videos through dawg.city between March 8–15. We focused on viral clips making extraordinary claims: candidates dropping out, leaked debate footage, and "insider" revelations.

The results:

  • 17 videos scored 85%+ AI-generated
  • 9 videos scored 60–85% (borderline, likely AI-assisted)
  • 24 videos scored under 50% (likely authentic)

The Three Most Common Deepfake Tactics

  1. Candidate face swaps on generic bodies — A real candidate's face placed on a body double in a compromising or misleading situation. dawg.city average score for this category: 91%.
  2. Fake "leaked" debate clips — AI-generated footage designed to look like private debate preparation or backstage moments. The audio is often the giveaway — too clean, too flat.
  3. AI-generated crowd reactions — Manufactured footage of large crowds cheering or booing, used to falsely imply popular sentiment. Backgrounds scored 88%+ AI in our tests.

The Most Viral Case

One video claiming a major candidate had unexpectedly dropped out of the race reached 1.8 million views before YouTube removed it — 6 hours after it was posted.

dawg.city score: 96% AI-generated.

The video drove significant social media panic and was cited in 47 news articles before fact-checkers confirmed it was fake. The creator's account has since been suspended.

"Election deepfakes are the fastest-growing category of synthetic media abuse we track. The 2026 cycle is unlike anything we've seen before." — AI integrity researcher

How to Protect Your Vote (and Your Feed)

  • Paste any suspicious political video into dawg.city before sharing
  • Check the channel age — most deepfake accounts are less than 30 days old
  • Look for mainstream news coverage before believing extraordinary claims
  • Report suspected deepfakes to YouTube using the "Misleading content" flag
🗳️ Election season rule: If a political video makes a shocking claim and has fewer than 10k views on a new channel, scan it before sharing. Every share of a deepfake extends its reach.
